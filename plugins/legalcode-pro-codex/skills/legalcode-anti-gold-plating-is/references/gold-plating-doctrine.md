## 1. Definitions and Doctrinal Framework

### 1.1 The phenomenon

**Gold-plating** (Icelandic: **gullhúðun**; Swedish: *guldplätering*; German: *Übererfüllung* / *überschießende Umsetzung*; Dutch: *nationale koppen*) is a metaphor for transposition practice in which a Member State (or, in Iceland's case, an EEA EFTA State) implements an EU directive in a manner more onerous than the directive minimally requires.

It is **not per se unlawful** — directives that contain minimum-harmonisation clauses expressly preserve Member-State competence to go further — but it is recognised across the EU/EEA institutional landscape as a regulatory-quality concern that demands **transparent justification**.

The phenomenon is distinct from:

- **non-transposition** (failure to implement);
- **late transposition** (implementing after the deadline);
- **incorrect transposition** (mis-transposition that produces non-conformity);
- **double-banking** (failing to repeal pre-existing parallel national law that overlaps the directive — Davidson Review terminology);
- **regulatory creep** (over-zealous enforcement of properly-transposed rules — also Davidson Review).

### 1.2 The European Commission's own usage (Better Regulation Toolbox, 2023/2025)

The Commission's *Better Regulation Toolbox* uses "gold-plating" in two distinct technical senses:

**(a) Competitiveness check (Internal Market chapter):**

> "Is there 'gold plating' – Member States adding national/local rules to the harmonised EU ones? If so, what are the reasons? Are there rules in other fields that affect the area under consideration? Could all these additional rules discourage cross-border activity?"

**(b) Administrative-burden methodology (Standard Cost Model section):**

> "If the obligation is set in EU Directive, i.e. requires transposition by Member States and they do not limit themselves to what is needed to meet the obligation (by 'gold-plating'), attribute only the percentage of costs resulting from the obligation set in the EU legislation. 'Gold-plating' in the case of administrative obligations refers, among other things, to **increasing the reporting frequency, to add 'data requirements' or to widen the target groups**."

**(c) Instrument choice:**

> "Regulations can limit the risk of 'gold-plating' associated with the transposition by Member States of Directives."

There is **no single Commission "Tool #29" devoted exclusively to gold-plating**. Treatment is distributed across the Toolbox.

### 1.3 The Davidson Review (UK, 2006) — the canonical taxonomy

The most-cited operational definition is from Lord Davidson's *Davidson Review – Final Report* (HMSO, November 2006), Chapter 2, §2.1:

> "**Gold-plating is when implementation goes beyond the minimum necessary to comply with the requirements of European legislation by:**
> - **extending the scope, adding in some way to the substantive requirement, or substituting wider UK legal terms for those used in the directive; or**
> - **not taking full advantage of any derogations which keep requirements to a minimum (e.g. for certain scales of operation, or specific activities); or**
> - **providing sanctions, enforcement mechanisms and matters such as burden of proof which go beyond the minimum needed (e.g. as a result of picking up the existing criminal sanctions in that area); or**
> - **implementing early, before the date given in the directive.**"

The Davidson Review formally lists **four** categories. The "Davidson Five" frequently cited in policy literature adds, as a separate fifth limb (Davidson §2.28):

> "(5) Maintaining higher pre-existing national standards than those required by a new European directive in the same legislative area" — which Davidson treated as analytically adjacent but which the Icelandic, Swedish, Dutch and German policy frameworks all treat as gold-plating in practice.

### 1.4 The Swedish extension (Regelrådet / Atthoff & Wallgren)

Sweden's *Regelrådet* (Better Regulation Council) has added a **sixth limb**: **using the transposition vehicle to introduce purely domestic ("sär-svenska") rules that fall outside the directive's scope and aim**. The Icelandic equivalent is breach of the **hrein innleiðingarfrumvörp** principle ("pure implementing bills") — see §5.

### 1.5 The Dutch position — IAK and the "nationale kop"

The Netherlands operates the *Integraal Afwegingskader voor beleid en regelgeving* (IAK), which since 2010 has incorporated a centralised gold-plating policy. Dutch transposition practice distinguishes between the **directive obligation** and any **"nationale kop"** (national top-up). The Dutch Cabinet's standing rule is that a national top-up requires explicit ministerial justification and is to be reported separately in the *memorie van toelichting*.

### 1.6 The German principle — *Eins-zu-eins-Umsetzung*

Successive German federal governments (2014, 2018, 2021 coalition agreements) have committed to *Eins-zu-eins-Umsetzung* — "one-to-one transposition" — of EU directives. The principle is enforced procedurally by the *Nationaler Normenkontrollrat* (NKR), which scrutinises the *Gesetzesfolgenabschätzung* (regulatory impact assessment) accompanying every transposition bill for unjustified additions.

These four terms are plain Icelandic legal vocabulary that a non-specialist member of utanríkismálanefnd will read without difficulty, while preserving the legal precision (max-harm divergence is *ólögmætt*; min-harm divergence is *lögmætt en endurskoðunarhæft*). The English-calque vocabulary (*„hámark"*, *„sveigjanleikaklausa"*) crowds the table cells with words that read like literal translations of the EU-English jargon and obscure the legal distinction rather than illuminate it.

### 1.8 EEA-specific context: Articles 7 and Protocol 35

Iceland does not transpose EU directives directly; it transposes them via decisions of the **EEA Joint Committee** (JCD) that incorporate the underlying EU act into the relevant Annex of the EEA Agreement, after which the EFTA-side state has a defined window to transpose.

The framework rules are:

**EEA Agreement, Article 7:**
> "Acts referred to or contained in the Annexes to this Agreement or in decisions of the EEA Joint Committee shall be binding upon the Contracting Parties and be, or be made, part of their internal legal order as follows:
> (a) an act corresponding to an EEC regulation shall as such be made part of the internal legal order of the Contracting Parties;
> (b) an act corresponding to an EEC directive shall leave to the authorities of the Contracting Parties the choice of form and method of implementation."

Article 7(b) replicates the EU TFEU Article 288 formula and is the explicit textual basis for transposition discretion — and therefore the gateway through which gold-plating becomes possible.

**Protocol 35 to the EEA Agreement** requires EEA EFTA States to introduce, "for cases of possible conflicts between implemented EEA rules and other statutory provisions", a statutory provision to the effect that implemented EEA rules prevail. Iceland implements this through **Article 3 of Act No. 2/1993 on the European Economic Area**:

> "Skýra skal lög og reglur, að svo miklu leyti sem við á, til samræmis við EES-samninginn og þær reglur sem á honum byggja."
> ("Laws and regulations shall, to the extent appropriate, be interpreted in conformity with the EEA Agreement and the rules built thereon.")

This is the Icelandic statutory basis for the **principle of EEA-conform interpretation**, recognised by the EFTA Court as analogous to but distinct from the EU's principle of indirect effect.

The **principle of homogeneity** (Preamble and Article 6 EEA Agreement) requires that, so far as the EEA Agreement is identical in substance to corresponding provisions of EU law, it be interpreted "in conformity with the relevant rulings of the Court of Justice of the European Communities". For gold-plating analysis this is critical: the Icelandic implementing text must be read against not only the directive but against any CJEU jurisprudence interpreting it, which itself fixes the "minimum required" baseline.

The procedural consequence is that Iceland's gold-plating arises in a **two-stage transposition** (EU act → EEA Joint Committee decision → Icelandic act/regulation), and the second stage is where gold-plating is introduced. The legislative record at that stage — the *frumvarp*, *greinargerð*, *samanburðartafla*, *umsagnir*, *nefndarálit* — is therefore the principal evidentiary record for tracing it.

## 2. The Davidson Five — Verbatim Restated with Icelandic Application

| # | Category (Davidson 2006 §2.1; §2.28) | Typical Icelandic manifestation | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Extending scope / adding requirements / substituting wider legal terms** | Wider gildissvið in §1 of the act; broader definitions in skilgreiningarákvæði; substitution of *"sá sem"* for the directive's narrower target. | **Act No. 3/2006 on Annual Accounts (ársreikningalög)**, transposing NFRD (Dir. 2014/95/EU): directive minimum threshold for non-financial reporting **> 500 employees**; Iceland lowered to **> 250 employees**, materially widening the regulated population. |
| **2** | **Not taking full advantage of derogations / opt-outs / discretions** | Failing to use Member-State-options ("aðildarríki geta") spelled out in the directive's articles. | MiFID II transposition (**Act 115/2021**): Iceland declined **eleven** Member-State discretions; several declinations had the *de facto* effect of imposing the stricter default on Icelandic firms. |
| **3** | **Providing sanctions / enforcement / burden-of-proof / procedure stricter than the minimum needed** | Criminal sanctions where the directive contemplates administrative; reversed burden of proof; lower de minimis; stricter procedural prerequisites. | **Act No. 90/2018 on the Protection of Privacy** (Iceland's GDPR transposition) — Article 47 provides for **imprisonment of up to 3 years** in addition to administrative fines. GDPR Art. 84 leaves criminal sanctions to MS discretion; Iceland's choice is Davidson-3. |
| **4** | **Implementing earlier than the directive requires** | Bringing the Icelandic act into force before the directive's transposition deadline or the JCD's effective date. | Detection signal: compare *gildistökuákvæði* of the act with the deadline in the directive's final article and with the *gildistökudagur* of the JCD. |
| **5** | **Maintaining stricter pre-existing national rules where the directive permits relaxation** (Davidson §2.28 — formally adjacent, but treated as gold-plating in IS/SE/NL/DE practice) | Retaining a stricter pre-2018 Icelandic regime when a new directive lowers the EU baseline; failing to repeal *"heimasmíðuð"* provisions when consolidating. | Documented across Icelandic financial-market legislation by *Seðlabanki Íslands*; documented systemically by Dr. Margrét Einarsdóttir's 25 Jan 2024 report (11/27 environment-ministry bills — 41%). |

## 3. Extended Pattern Catalogue (from Squintani, Steunenberg & Voermans, Mastenbroek, the 2024 task-force report, *Seðlabanki* commentary, and SI/SA/SFF/Viðskiptaráð consultation submissions)

### A. Scope-and-coverage manipulations
1. **Lowering numerical thresholds** (employee headcount, turnover, balance-sheet total) — NFRD/CSRD example above.
2. **Expanding personal scope** — substituting the directive's restricted addressee (e.g. "credit institution") with a broader Icelandic term (e.g. "fjármálafyrirtæki").
3. **Expanding material scope** — covering products, services, transactions outside the directive's annex.
4. **Removing exclusions / carve-outs** — directive excludes micro-enterprises; Icelandic act omits the exclusion.
5. **Substituting wider legal terms** — using *"upplýsingar"* (information) where the directive uses the narrower *"personal data"*.

### B. Discretion and option mishandling
6. **Declining permissive derogations** ("Member States may exempt…").
7. **Reading discretion against the regulated party** — choosing the more burdensome of two permitted interpretations.
8. **Removing safe harbours** that the directive expressly preserves.
9. **Tightening "comply or explain" into mandatory "comply"**.
10. **Removing private enforcement caps** the directive permits.

### C. Procedural and evidentiary tightening
11. **Requiring prior authorisation** where directive requires only **notification**.
12. **Requiring written / notarised form** where directive is technology-neutral.
13. **Reversing the burden of proof** — onus on the regulated entity where the directive places it on the authority.
14. **Reducing transitional / grandfathering periods** below directive defaults.
15. **Lowering de minimis thresholds** (penalty floors, notification thresholds, materiality thresholds).
16. **Tightening filing frequencies** (quarterly where directive says annual).

### D. Sanctions and enforcement
17. **Criminalising what the directive treats as administrative**.
18. **Higher administrative fine caps** than the directive's "effective, proportionate, dissuasive" floor.
19. **Joint and several liability** where the directive contemplates individual liability.
20. **Personal liability of directors** beyond directive minimum.
21. **Adding fee / levy regimes** to fund the supervisor beyond directive specifications.

### E. Drafting-technique patterns
22. **"Belt-and-suspenders"** — restating the directive obligation and adding a parallel Icelandic obligation covering the same conduct.
23. **Substantive elaboration in subordinate legislation** (*reglugerð*) that the directive does not require.
24. **Definite-article tightening** — *"the data subject"* becomes *"hvern þann sem upplýsingarnar varða"*, subtly widening reach.
25. **Removing flexibility verbs** — deleting "where appropriate", "if necessary", "subject to proportionality", "to the extent possible".
26. **Future-mandatory tense** — Icelandic future-shall constructions for what the directive frames as conditional.

### F. Cross-border / single-market consequences
27. **Tighter cross-border restrictions** (potential issue under TFEU Art. 36 / EEA Art. 13).
28. **Imposing host-State requirements** that go beyond passport-recognition the directive grants.
29. **Country-of-origin overrides** — refusing mutual recognition the directive establishes.

### G. Sär-svenska / heimasmíðuð additions (Regelrådet sixth limb)
30. **Using the transposition bill as a vehicle for unrelated domestic reforms** — breach of *hrein innleiðingarfrumvörp* (Art. 8(2nd para) of the Parliamentary EEA Procedure Rules; §13(3) of the Cabinet Resolution on Government Bills).

Squintani additionally distinguishes **gold-plating** (extension that increases burden) from **green-plating** (extension that raises environmental ambition); both are forms of "umfram lágmark" and must be flagged, though policy assessment differs.

## 4. Linguistic / Word-Choice Signals — the Diagnostic Vocabulary

This is the heart of the section-by-section diagnostic. Compare the Icelandic implementing-act wording against (a) the directive's English text and (b) the **official Icelandic translation** of the directive published in the EES-viðbætir.

### 4.1 Modal verbs — the mandatory/permissive axis

| Directive (EN) | Directive (typical IS translation) | Mandatory/permissive | Red flag if IS implementing act uses… |
|---|---|---|---|
| *shall* | **skal** | mandatory | — (matches) |
| *must* | **ber að** | mandatory | — (matches) |
| *may* | **er heimilt** / **má** | permissive | **skal** or **ber að** — converts option into obligation (Davidson-2) |
| *should* | **ætti að** | hortatory | **skal** — hardens recital-style nudge into binding rule |
| *is required to* | **er skylt að** | mandatory | — (matches) |
| *may not / shall not* | **er óheimilt** | prohibitive | — (matches) |
| *is prohibited from* | **er óheimilt** / **er bannað** | prohibitive | — (matches) |

The classic Icelandic gold-plating signature is **"skal"** where the directive says **"may"**. Conversely, **"er heimilt"** rendering a directive **"shall"** is *under*-implementation (a non-conformity, not gold-plating).

### 4.2 Quantifier and qualifier signals

| Directive (EN) | Permissive IS equivalent | Red flag if IS uses… |
|---|---|---|
| *where appropriate* | **eftir því sem við á** / **þegar við á** | unconditional formulation (no qualifier) |
| *where necessary* | **eftir þörfum** / **sé það nauðsynlegt** | **ávallt** (always); unconditional |
| *to the extent necessary* | **að því marki sem nauðsynlegt er** | unconditional / **að fullu** (in full) |
| *without prejudice to* | **með fyrirvara um** | omitted — silently overriding preserved regime |
| *as far as possible* | **eftir því sem unnt er** | unconditional |
| *at least* (min-harm marker) | **að minnsta kosti** | **eingöngu / aðeins** (only — converts floor to ceiling) |
| *at most / not exceeding* (max-harm marker) | **að hámarki / ekki meira en** | **að minnsta kosti** (inverts cap into floor — **unlawful gold-plating**) |
| *reasonable* | **sanngjarn** / **hæfilegur** | quantified floor/ceiling ("eigi minna en X kr.") |
| *proportionate* | **í réttu hlutfalli við** / **hóflegur** | absolute numerical threshold |
| *where relevant* | **eftir atvikum** | **í öllum tilvikum** (in all cases) |

### 4.3 Temporal signals

| Directive (EN) | Permissive IS | Red flag if IS uses… |
|---|---|---|
| *within a reasonable time* | **innan hæfilegs tíma** | **án tafar / tafarlaust** (without delay); fixed short deadline |
| *as soon as practicable* | **svo fljótt sem auðið er** | **þegar í stað** (immediately) |
| *no later than [date]* | **eigi síðar en [dagsetning]** | earlier IS date than directive deadline (Davidson-4) |
| *periodically* | **með reglubundnum hætti** | quarterly / monthly specifications |
| *annually* | **árlega** | **ársfjórðungslega** (quarterly) |

### 4.4 Personal-scope tighteners

| Directive (EN) | Permissive IS | Red flag if IS uses… |
|---|---|---|
| *the controller* / *the operator* | **ábyrgðaraðili** / **rekstraraðili** | **hver sá sem** / **sérhver** / **allir þeir sem** — widens to anyone |
| *concerned* | **viðkomandi** | **öllum** (everyone) |
| *natural or legal persons established in the Member State* | **einstaklingum eða lögaðilum með staðfestu í aðildarríki** | omission of "með staðfestu" — covers all persons globally |

### 4.5 Sanction / enforcement vocabulary

| Directive (EN) | Permissive IS | Red flag if IS uses… |
|---|---|---|
| *effective, proportionate and dissuasive* | **skilvirk, í réttu hlutfalli og letjandi** | hard-coded numerical floor ("eigi lægri en …") |
| *administrative sanctions* | **stjórnvaldssektir** / **stjórnsýsluviðurlög** | **refsing** / **fangelsi** / **sektir og fangelsi** — criminalisation (Davidson-3) |
| *MS may provide for criminal sanctions in addition* | **aðildarríki geta kveðið á um refsiviðurlög til viðbótar** | mandatory criminalisation — converts option to obligation |
| *natural and legal persons* (sanction targets) | **einstaklingar og lögaðilar** | personal director liability where directive doesn't require it |

### 4.6 Connectors that signal piggy-backed obligations

The IS textual markers that *frequently introduce a gold-plated addition*:

- **"auk þess"** — "in addition"
- **"enn fremur"** — "furthermore"
- **"jafnframt"** — "at the same time"
- **"þá skal einnig"** — "also there shall…"
- **"að uppfylltum eftirfarandi skilyrðum"** followed by conditions not in the directive
- **"þó skal"** — pseudo-exception that tightens

Each warrants a check against the directive: does the directive contemplate the *added* obligation?

### 4.7 Definite-article and scope-creep signals

A subtle but real Icelandic pattern: the directive narrows scope through indefinite or restrictive constructions; the Icelandic transposition silently broadens via definite article or universal quantifier. Examples:

- Directive: *"a competent authority"* → IS: *"lögbært stjórnvald"* (matches) vs *"öll lögbær stjórnvöld"* (widens).
- Directive: *"affected customers"* → IS: *"viðskiptavinir sem málið varðar"* (matches) vs *"allir viðskiptavinir"* (widens).

### 4.8 EU drafters' standard phrases — the flexibility lexicon

When the **directive** uses these phrases it is signalling *flexibility that the transposing state is entitled to preserve*. Their **disappearance** from the Icelandic implementing text is a gold-plating signal:

- *"Member States may"* — opt-in discretion.
- *"Member States shall ensure that … by appropriate means"* — choice of mechanism is preserved.
- *"where appropriate"* / *"as appropriate"* — proportionality qualifier.
- *"without prejudice to"* — preserves other regimes; deleting overrides them.
- *"to the extent necessary"* — necessity qualifier.
- *"in accordance with national law and practice"* — preserves domestic procedure.
- *"taking into account the specific characteristics of small and medium-sized undertakings"* — SME-sensitivity clause.
- *"at least"* (in a list of minimum requirements) — confirms minimum harmonisation; states may exceed but must at least implement these.

## 5. Pre-Law Detection Methodology

### 5.1 The statutory framework requiring disclosure

Three instruments together require **explicit gold-plating disclosure** in the explanatory memorandum (*greinargerð*):

**(i) Article 8(b) of the Parliamentary EEA Procedure Rules** (*Reglur forsætisnefndar Alþingis um þinglega meðferð EES-mála*), as quoted in the Foreign Ministry's 31 May 2024 task-force report:

> *"Í greinargerð með viðkomandi lagafrumvarpi skulu eftirfarandi efnisatriði koma fram: […] b. Að hvaða marki frumvarpið hafi að geyma frávik frá upphaflegu ESB-gerðinni. Sérstaklega skal tilgreint ef gengið er lengra en lágmarksákvæði viðkomandi gerðar kveður á um og skal rökstuðningur fylgja slíkri ákvörðun. Tilgreina ber sérstaklega ef svigrúm er til staðar við innleiðingu."*

> Translation: "The explanatory memorandum for the bill must include the following: … (b) the extent to which the bill departs from the original EU act. In particular, it must be stated if the bill goes further than the minimum provisions of the act, and reasons must be given for that decision. It must also be stated specifically whether implementation discretion was available."

**(ii) Article 37(1), second sentence, of Act No. 55/1991 on the Parliamentary Procedure of Althingi**:

> *"Frumvörp, sem innleiða eiga reglur er byggjast á EES-gerðum, skulu að meginstefnu ekki fela í sér önnur ákvæði en þau sem leiðir af gerðinni og skal í greinargerð tilgreina hvaða gerðir frumvarpinu er ætlað að innleiða og taka fram ef gengið er lengra en lágmarkskröfur gerða kveða á um."*

This is the statutory anchor of the **hrein innleiðingarfrumvörp** principle and the disclosure obligation.

**(iii) Article 13(3) of the Government Resolution on the Preparation and Processing of Government Bills**:

> *"Frumvörp til innleiðingar á EES-gerð skulu að meginstefnu til aðeins fela í sér þær breytingar sem nauðsynlegar eru til að endurspegla þá EES-skuldbindingu sem við á. Varði frumvarpið fleiri atriði en innleiðingu gerðar skal í greinargerð tilgreina sérstaklega hvaða greinar frumvarpsins eru til innleiðingar auk þess sem rökstyðja ber hvers vegna talið var nauðsynlegt að víkja frá meginreglum um hrein innleiðingarfrumvörp."*

### 5.2 What to read in the legislative record

| Artefact | Where found | What to look for |
|---|---|---|
| **Frumvarp** (bill text) | althingi.is – mál number | Compare every article against the directive's corresponding article. |
| **Greinargerð – almenn ákvæði** | Same | Look for the explicit gold-plating disclosure required by §8(b). Absence = red flag. |
| **Athugasemdir við einstakar greinar** | Same | Each article's commentary should state which directive article it transposes and flag any departure. |
| **Samanburðartafla** | Fylgiskjal to frumvarp; sometimes only in samráðsgátt draft | Tabulates directive article → IS article. **Most direct evidence of gold-plating** — divergence visible in side-by-side columns. |
| **Mat á áhrifum (RIA)** | Fylgiskjal | Cost-benefit basis for any deviation. Thin or missing RIA on a divergent provision = red flag. |
| **Samráðsgátt drög and umsagnir** | island.is/samradsgatt | Interest-group consultation responses frequently flag gold-plating. Trade associations (SA, SI, SFF, FA, Viðskiptaráð) routinely catalogue divergences. |
| **Nefndarálit + breytingatillögur** | althingi.is | Gold-plating is sometimes *added* at committee stage without RIA — particularly dangerous. |
| **EEA Joint Committee decision** | efta.int / EES-viðbætir | Fixes the binding text and the transposition window. |
| **Official IS translation in EES-viðbætir** | efta.int | The authoritative Icelandic text of the directive — the proper textual benchmark. |

### 5.3 The 2007 Handbók um undirbúning og frágang lagafrumvarpa

§3.7 requires that when implementing an EEA act, the act must contain an express implementing clause referring to the underlying EU act *and* to the relevant EEA Joint Committee decision (not merely mention it in the greinargerð). Its **absence** is a procedural non-conformity flag independent of gold-plating.

§4.2 requires that the article-by-article commentary explain what is changing and how individual elements are to be understood. The Handbók specifically warns against the comment "thar fnast ekki skýringar" ("requires no explanation") for transposition provisions. A bare "needs no explanation" comment on a substantive divergence is a process red flag.

### 5.4 The "hrein innleiðingarfrumvörp" filter

If the implementing bill **also makes domestic-only changes**, the greinargerð must:
1. identify which articles are pure transposition and which are domestic;
2. justify why the domestic content was bundled with the transposition.

A reviewer flags as gold-plating-adjacent any provision in an "implementing bill" that does not trace back to an article of the directive.

### 5.5 Disclosure classification

A finding has three possible characterisations:

1. **Disclosed and justified** — §8(b) disclosure plus an RIA-supported rationale. Lawful and policy-defensible.
2. **Disclosed but unjustified** — flagged but with no substantive rationale beyond restating policy preference. Lawful but vulnerable.
3. **Undisclosed** — divergence exists but the greinargerð is silent. Breach of §8(b) and §37(1)/2 ÞSL; presumptively unlawful as a procedural matter.

The 25 January 2024 Environment Ministry report (Dr. Margrét Einarsdóttir) found that in approximately **half** of the 11 gold-plated bills in its 27-bill sample (2010–2022) the divergence was disclosed; in the other half it was undisclosed and required substantial reverse-engineering to identify.

## 6. Documented Icelandic Gold-Plating Case Studies

### 6.1 Non-Financial Reporting (NFRD → Act No. 3/2006 on Annual Accounts)
- **Directive**: 2014/95/EU amending Directive 2013/34/EU
- **Directive threshold**: large public-interest entities with **> 500 employees**
- **Icelandic threshold**: **> 250 employees**
- **Davidson category**: 1 (extending scope by lowering numerical threshold)

### 6.2 Data Protection (GDPR → Act No. 90/2018 on the Protection of Privacy)
- **Instrument**: Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (a regulation, but with extensive Member-State discretion)
- **Gold-plating**: Article 47 layers **criminal sanctions of up to 3 years' imprisonment** on top of the GDPR administrative-fine regime. GDPR Art. 84 leaves criminal sanctions to MS discretion; Iceland's choice is **Davidson-3** sanctions escalation.

### 6.3 MiFID II → Act No. 115/2021 on Markets in Financial Instruments
- **Directive**: 2014/65/EU (MiFID II) + MiFIR Reg. 600/2014
- **Pattern**: Declination of **eleven** Member-State discretions, several with the effect of imposing stricter defaults on Icelandic firms; layering of IS-specific penalty provisions.

### 6.4 Pharmacy real-time stock disclosure (153rd parliament, bill 938)
- Bill required **real-time** disclosure of stock levels by marketing-authorisation holders, manufacturers, wholesalers, hospitals and pharmacies to the Icelandic Medicines Agency.
- *Félag atvinnurekenda* (FA) submission noted "nowhere in the European regulation is such real-time access prescribed", calling it a "very crude example of gold-plating".

### 6.5 Environment, Energy and Climate Ministry portfolio (2010–2022) — systemic finding
- **Scope**: 27 transposition bills surveyed.
- **Finding**: gold-plating present in **11/27 (41%)**.
- In bills *implementing directives*: **4/8** contained gold-plating.
- In bills *implementing regulations*: **0/8** (as expected — transposition discretion is minimal).
- **Disclosure rate**: ~half disclosed; ~half undisclosed.

## 7. Detection Heuristics — Reviewer Checklist (35 items)

Run **per article**, plus a whole-bill pass.

### Stage A — Identification (once per bill)
1. Identify the underlying EU/EEA act(s) and the JCD incorporating each.
2. Obtain the official IS translation from EES-viðbætir (do not re-translate).
3. Confirm the directive's harmonisation level per article: min / max / blended. (Absence of min-harm clause + absence of "stricter rules permitted" → presumption of maximum harmonisation per CJEU C-52/00 *Commission v France*, in which case gold-plating is **unlawful**.)
4. Locate or construct a samanburðartafla.
5. Locate the §8(b) gold-plating disclosure in the greinargerð. Absent or vague = elevated scrutiny.

### Stage B — Scope and definitions (Davidson-1 / Squintani extension)
6. Does §1 (*gildissvið*) match the directive's scope article?
7. Do the definitions in §2 (*skilgreiningar*) match the directive's definitions?
8. Are numerical thresholds (employees, turnover, asset size) identical? Lower = Davidson-1.
9. Are exclusions/carve-outs retained? Omitted = Davidson-1.
10. Are SME-sensitivity clauses preserved? Deleted = Davidson-1.

### Stage C — Discretion handling (Davidson-2)
11. For every "Member States may" in the directive, identify Iceland's choice. Did Iceland decline a burden-reducing option?
12. For every "where appropriate / if necessary / to the extent necessary" qualifier, is the Icelandic equivalent preserved?
13. For every safe harbour/defence the directive permits, is it preserved?
14. For every "comply or explain", has Iceland tightened it to mandatory comply?

### Stage D — Procedural and evidentiary (Davidson-3 / Pattern C)
15. Does directive require *notification* but IS act require *prior authorisation*?
16. Does IS act require written/notarised form where directive is technology-neutral?
17. Has burden of proof been reversed against the regulated party?
18. Are transitional periods shorter than directive's?
19. Are filing frequencies tighter (quarterly where directive says annual)?
20. Are de minimis thresholds lower?

### Stage E — Sanctions (Davidson-3)
21. Has Iceland imposed criminal sanctions where directive contemplates only administrative?
22. Are administrative-fine caps higher than directive's floor?
23. Is there personal director liability beyond directive requirements?
24. Are there fee/levy regimes funding the supervisor not contemplated by directive?

### Stage F — Temporal (Davidson-4)
25. Does the gildistökuákvæði bring IS act into force *before* the directive's deadline or the JCD's effective date?

### Stage G — Pre-existing stricter rules (Davidson-5)
26. Were there pre-existing IS rules in this area? Has the new act removed, retained, or tightened them?
27. If retained: does the greinargerð address the retention?

### Stage H — Drafting-vocabulary scan (per article)
28. **skal** vs **má / heimilt** — does each IS modal match the directive's?
29. **án tafar / tafarlaust / ávallt** — stronger than directive's "within reasonable time / where necessary"?
30. **að minnsta kosti** in a max-harm context, or **að hámarki** in a min-harm context — sign of inversion.
31. **óheimilt** where directive says "may be restricted" — converts permission into prohibition.
32. **auk þess / enn fremur / jafnframt / þá skal einnig** — connectors introducing add-ons; check directive.
33. Universal quantifiers (**öllum / sérhver / hver sá sem**) replacing directive's specific addressee.
34. Removal of flexibility clauses (*eftir því sem við á*, *eftir atvikum*, *eftir þörfum*, *eftir því sem unnt er*, *með fyrirvara um*) — silent rigidification.

### Stage I — Vehicle integrity (Pattern G — Regelrådet sixth limb)
35. Does the bill contain provisions unrelated to the directive (breach of *hrein innleiðingarfrumvörp*)? If so, are they identified and justified in the greinargerð?

**Output per finding**: (a) Davidson category + (b) Pattern letter + (c) Disclosure status (disclosed-and-justified / disclosed-but-unjustified / undisclosed) + (d) Severity + (e) Confidence.
